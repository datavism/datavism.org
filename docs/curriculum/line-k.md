## 0. Executive Summary

LINE K is the first major investigative specialization after LINE G.

LINE G teaches the method:

> Question → Command → Intake → Verification → Evaluation

LINE K applies that method to tracking, surveillance, data brokers, identity systems and OSINT.

It is the line that most directly fulfills the original DATAVISM hook:

> They track you. Learn to track back.

But LINE K is not a course in paranoia.  
It is not a hacker fantasy.  
It is not about doxxing people or turning surveillance back onto individuals.

LINE K is about making observation systems observable.

It teaches Datavists to identify digital traces, read technical signals, understand identity graphs, monitor change over time and turn tracking structures into public evidence.

Its final output is the **Panopticon Case File**.

---

## 1. Role in the Evidence Engine

DATAVISM is an Evidence Engine.

Every line teaches a different way of turning hidden systems into public evidence.

LINE K turns surveillance into something traceable.

It answers:

- What traces are produced?
- Who receives them?
- How do technical signals become profiles?
- Which actors assemble, enrich or sell those profiles?
- What can be proven?
- What remains uncertain?
- How can the result be published without harming individuals?

LINE K should be the first thematic line to receive deep development because it connects:

- personal experience,
- technical forensics,
- AI-era literacy,
- public-interest investigation,
- data activism,
- and the DATAVISM storyworld.

- requests,
- scripts,
- tags,
- pixels,
- redirects,
- IDs,
- categories,
- brokers,
- policies,
- records,
- changes over time.

KEY is precision, not spectacle.

## Line Motto

> **Trace the watchers.**

## Expanded Motto

> They track you. LINE K teaches you to track the system back.

## Core Question

> **Who watches us, and how?**

## System / Opponent

**PANOPTICON**

PANOPTICON is not one company, one state agency or one villain.

PANOPTICON is a system logic:

## 3. Ethical Boundary

LINE K needs a stronger ethical frame than any other line.

Because OSINT and tracking analysis can easily become invasive, irresponsible or performative.

The canonical rule:

> **We trace systems, not private individuals.**

LINE K investigates:

- platforms,
- companies,
- adtech systems,
- data brokers,
- consent flows,
- public databases,
- technical infrastructure,
- policies,
- tracking mechanisms,
- institutional actors,
- commercial surveillance systems.

LINE K does **not** investigate private individuals as targets.

## Not Allowed

LINE K must not become:

- doxxing,
- stalking,
- deanonymization of private people,
- credential attacks,
- account compromise,
- harassment,
- publishing sensitive personal data,
- “OSINT flexing”,
- vigilante surveillance,
- illegal access,
- bypassing authentication,
- targeting vulnerable people.

## Allowed Direction

LINE K should focus on:

- public-interest systems,
- corporate or institutional infrastructures,
- tracking patterns,
- adtech ecosystems,
- public records,
- aggregate evidence,
- consent mechanisms,
- data flows,
- transparency databases,
- system behavior over time.

## Principle

> If a method would feel abusive when turned against a private person, it does not belong in LINE K.

## 4. Artistic Research Frame

LINE K is especially strong as artistic research because tracking is normally invisible.

People feel watched, scored, sorted and targeted — but they rarely see the machinery.

The artistic-research question is:

# 6. K1 — FOOTPRINTS

## Role in the Line

K1 is the orientation station.

Before learners can investigate tracking systems, they need to understand what a digital trace is.

K1 shifts the learner from:

> “I am being tracked.”

to:

> “Which traces exist, where are they produced, and who might receive them?”

This station should be accessible, personal and unsettling — but not sensationalist.

## Core Question

> What traces exist before anyone calls them data?

## Learner Outcome

After K1, the learner can:

- name common types of digital traces;
- distinguish voluntary, behavioral, technical and inferred traces;
- map where traces are produced;
- describe who might receive or process them;
- assess which traces may be sensitive;
- formulate a first tracking-related investigation question.

## Key Concepts

- Digital traces
- First-party vs. third-party context
- Behavioral data
- Technical metadata
- Device identifiers
- Browser signals
- Location traces
- Account traces
- Payment traces
- Search and interaction traces
- Inferred data
- Trace vs. profile

## Suggested Signal / Opening

A learner opens a normal day:

- checks weather,
- opens a messenger,
- reads a news article,
- searches for a symptom,
- buys something,
- scrolls a feed,
- watches a video,
- signs up for a newsletter.

The station asks:

> What traces did this day produce?

## Method

Trace inventory.

The learner creates a structured inventory of trace types.

## Tooling

Possible tools:

- browser history export or manual reconstruction,
- spreadsheet,
- privacy label / app permission pages,
- browser dev tools for basic observation,
- AI co-pilot to categorize trace types,
- manual diary of digital actions.

No invasive tooling required.

## Artifact

**Footprint Map**

Minimum fields:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Action | What happened? |
| Trace Type | What kind of trace might be produced? |
| Context | Browser, app, platform, payment, location, search etc. |
| Possible Receiver | First party, third party, broker, analytics provider, platform |
| Sensitivity | Low / medium / high |
| Visibility | Can the user see it? |
| Evidence | Screenshot, source, documentation, observed request, policy |
| Open Question | What needs further investigation? |

## Self-Check

- [ ] I can explain the difference between an action and a trace.
- [ ] I can list at least five trace types.
- [ ] I can identify possible receivers of a trace.
- [ ] I can mark which claims are observed and which are assumptions.
- [ ] I can formulate one follow-up question for K2.

## Station Sentence

> Before you can trace the watchers, you need to understand the traces.

# 7. K2 — SIGNALS

## Role in the Line

K2 is where LINE K becomes visibly technical.

The learner moves from abstract footprint awareness to observable tracking signals.

K2 should teach people that tracking is not magic and not purely hidden. It often leaves marks:

- network requests,
- scripts,
- cookies,
- pixels,
- SDK references,
- redirects,
- consent events,
- URL parameters,
- third-party domains.

## Core Question

> Which technical signals prove that tracking is happening?

## Learner Outcome

After K2, the learner can:

- inspect a website or public interface for tracking signals;
- identify common tracking mechanisms;
- distinguish observation from inference;
- document technical evidence without overclaiming;
- create a Tracking Signal Sheet.

## Key Concepts

- Cookies
- Tracking pixels
- Third-party scripts
- Tag managers
- SDKs
- Network requests
- Referrers
- UTM parameters
- Consent Management Platforms
- Client-side vs. server-side tracking
- Event payloads
- Domain ownership uncertainty
- HAR files as evidence
- Screenshot vs. technical record

## Suggested Signal / Opening

Open a public webpage with browser dev tools.

Ask:

> What happens before you even click?

Show:

- third-party domains,
- analytics scripts,
- consent banner behavior,
- network requests,
- cookies set before or after consent,
- marketing tags.

## Method

Signal inspection.

The learner observes technical signals and documents them as evidence.

## Tooling

Possible tools:

- Browser DevTools Network tab,
- browser privacy extensions for observation,
- HAR export,
- cookie inspector,
- public tag scanner tools,
- spreadsheet,
- AI co-pilot to classify domains or summarize requests,
- screenshot annotation.

## Artifact

**Tracking Signal Sheet**

Minimum fields:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Page / Context | Where was the signal observed? |
| Timestamp | When was it observed? |
| Signal Type | Cookie, request, script, pixel, SDK, redirect etc. |
| Domain / Actor | Which domain or actor appears? |
| Evidence | Screenshot, HAR line, request URL, cookie name etc. |
| Observed Purpose | What appears to happen? |
| Claimed Purpose | What the site/policy says, if available |
| Confidence | High / medium / low |
| Uncertainty | What cannot be concluded yet? |

## Verification

Minimum verification:

1. Observe the request or signal.
2. Capture evidence.
3. Check whether the domain/provider is identifiable.
4. Compare against public documentation or privacy policy.
5. Mark uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] I can show at least one observed tracking signal.
- [ ] I can distinguish a technical observation from a claim about intent.
- [ ] I can name the evidence I captured.
- [ ] I can explain what I do **not** know yet.
- [ ] I can connect the signal back to a K1 footprint.

## Station Sentence

> Tracking is not invisible. It leaves signals.

# 8. K3 — IDENTITY GRAPH

## Role in the Line

K3 is the conceptual center of LINE K.

K1 shows traces.  
K2 shows signals.  
K3 shows how traces and signals can become profiles.

This is where learners understand that a profile is not a person.

A profile is an assembled claim.

## Core Question

> How does a scattered trace become a profile?

## Learner Outcome

After K3, the learner can:

- explain how identifiers connect traces;
- distinguish deterministic and probabilistic identity logic at a high level;
- understand segments, categories and inferred traits;
- map actors involved in identity construction;
- identify where uncertainty, harm or misclassification can enter;
- create an Identity Graph Sketch.

## Key Concepts

- Identifier
- Cookie ID
- Mobile Ad ID
- Hashed email
- Login ID
- Device graph
- Customer Data Platform
- Data Management Platform
- Data broker
- Segment
- Attribute
- Inference
- Lookalike audience
- Matching
- Enrichment
- False positives
- Sensitive category risk
- Profile as claim

## Core Principle

> The profile is not a fact. It is an assembled claim.

## Suggested Signal / Opening

Start with a simple chain:

```text
Website visit → cookie ID → interest segment → ad targeting
```

Then complicate it:

```text
Newsletter signup → hashed email → platform match → custom audience → lookalike segment
```

Then ask:

> Where in this chain can error, harm or power enter?

## Method

Identity graph sketching.

The learner models how data points can move from trace to identifier to segment to use case.

## Tooling

Possible tools:

- whiteboard or diagram tool,
- spreadsheet,
- AI co-pilot for graph cleanup,
- public adtech documentation,
- transparency databases,
- data broker category examples,
- manual source collection.

## Artifact

**Identity Graph Sketch**

Minimum structure:

```text
Signal → Identifier → Actor → Segment / Claim → Use Case → Risk
```

Example:

```text
Website visit
→ cookie ID
→ ad network
→ “interest: financial stress”
→ targeting / exclusion / pricing
→ discrimination or manipulation risk
```

## Artifact Fields

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Signal | The observed or plausible trace |
| Identifier | Cookie, email hash, account ID, device ID etc. |
| Actor | Platform, broker, advertiser, vendor |
| Transformation | Match, infer, enrich, segment, score |
| Claim | What the system appears to believe |
| Use Case | Targeting, personalization, scoring, exclusion etc. |
| Risk | Harm, error, asymmetry, opacity |
| Evidence | Source or observed proof |
| Confidence | High / medium / low |

## Verification

The learner must mark which parts are:

- observed,
- documented,
- inferred,
- speculative,
- unknown.

This is essential. K3 must not teach people to draw scary diagrams without evidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] I can explain why a profile is not the same as a person.
- [ ] I can map at least one signal-to-segment chain.
- [ ] I can identify where inference enters the system.
- [ ] I can mark evidence vs. speculation.
- [ ] I can name at least one risk created by identity graph logic.

## Station Sentence

> The profile is not a fact. It is an assembled claim.

# 9. K4 — WATCHTOWER

## Role in the Line

K4 shifts the learner from one-time analysis to ongoing investigation.

Many systems reveal themselves through change:

- policies change,
- scripts appear or disappear,
- trackers are added,
- apps request new permissions,
- ad libraries expose new campaigns,
- transparency databases receive new records,
- corporate structures shift,
- public claims change.

K4 teaches the practice of watching.

## Core Question

> What changes when nobody is looking?

## Learner Outcome

After K4, the learner can:

- define a monitoring target;
- identify which signals matter over time;
- choose a responsible monitoring cadence;
- document changes;
- distinguish meaningful change from noise;
- create a Watchlist / Monitor.

## Key Concepts

- Monitoring
- Change detection
- Watchlist
- Baseline
- Drift
- Alert
- Signal vs. noise
- Cadence
- Public source monitoring
- Versioning
- Evidence log
- Responsible OSINT
- Rate limits and terms of service

## Suggested Signal / Opening

Pick one public target:

- a privacy policy,
- a vendor list,
- an app permission page,
- a public ad library search,
- a transparency database query,
- a public company page,
- a GitHub changelog,
- a website tag configuration,
- a public procurement record.

Ask:

> What would be meaningful if it changed?

## Method

Watchtower monitoring.

The learner defines a baseline and creates a change log.

## Tooling

Possible tools:

- manual periodic checks,
- visual diff tools,
- RSS or alert tools,
- simple scripts,
- website change detection services,
- public databases,
- spreadsheets,
- AI co-pilot for summarizing diffs,
- versioned notes.

## Artifact

**Watchlist / Monitor**

Minimum fields:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Target | What is being watched? |
| Why It Matters | Public-interest reason |
| Source URL / Access | Where the signal comes from |
| Baseline | Current observed state |
| Change Signal | What kind of change matters? |
| Cadence | How often to check |
| Method | Manual, alert, script, API etc. |
| Evidence Log | Date, change, source, screenshot, note |
| Risk / Ethics | Why this monitoring is responsible |
| Next Action | What happens if something changes? |

## Verification

Each change must be:

- timestamped,
- source-linked,
- captured,
- compared to baseline,
- interpreted cautiously.

## Self-Check

- [ ] I can define a monitoring target.
- [ ] I can explain why the target matters.
- [ ] I can describe what change would be meaningful.
- [ ] I can document change without overclaiming.
- [ ] I can respect legal, ethical and rate-limit boundaries.

## Station Sentence

> Watch the system long enough, and it starts to reveal its habits.

# 10. K5 — PANOPTICON FILE

## Role in the Line

K5 is the culmination of LINE K.

The learner turns the previous artifacts into a public evidence package.

K1 produced the Footprint Map.  
K2 produced the Tracking Signal Sheet.  
K3 produced the Identity Graph Sketch.  
K4 produced the Watchlist / Monitor.

K5 turns them into a **Panopticon Case File**.

## Core Question

> How do we make a surveillance structure visible and verifiable?

## Learner Outcome

After K5, the learner can:

- formulate a tracking/surveillance investigation question;
- document sources and signals;
- explain identity or tracking logic;
- separate proof from inference;
- identify risks and harms;
- produce a publishable Case File.

## Key Concepts

- Case File
- Public evidence
- Source trail
- Claim discipline
- Harm framing
- Uncertainty
- Responsible disclosure
- Redaction
- Reproducibility
- Public-interest narrative
- Visual explanation

## Artifact

**Panopticon Case File**

Canonical structure:

```md
# Panopticon Case File — [Title]

## 1. Question

What tracking or surveillance structure are we investigating?

## 2. Context

Why does this matter?

## 3. System / Actor

Which platform, company, vendor, public institution, broker or infrastructure is involved?

## 4. Sources

What documents, signals, databases, policies, pages, requests or public records were used?

## 5. Footprints

Which traces are relevant?

## 6. Signals

Which technical signals were observed?

## 7. Identity Logic

How might traces become identifiers, segments, profiles or claims?

## 8. Monitoring Evidence

What changed over time, if anything?

## 9. Findings

What can we responsibly claim?

## 10. Limits

What can we not prove?

## 11. Risks / Harms

Who or what may be affected?

## 12. Public Output

How is the finding communicated?
Report, visualization, poster, dataset, essay, tool, archive?

## 13. Next Questions

What should be investigated next?
```

## Verification Requirements

A Panopticon Case File must include:

- at least one primary source or observed technical signal;
- clear distinction between evidence and inference;
- screenshot or source trail where appropriate;
- uncertainty markers;
- no publication of sensitive personal data;
- no claims about intent without evidence;
- no naming of private individuals as targets.

## Self-Check

- [ ] I can state the investigation question in one sentence.
- [ ] I can show the strongest evidence.
- [ ] I can explain the identity or tracking logic without overclaiming.
- [ ] I can name what remains uncertain.
- [ ] I can publish the finding without harming private individuals.

## Station Sentence

> A surveillance system becomes weaker when it becomes legible.
